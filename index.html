02 — India Manufacturing Landscape
India's $1.2 trillion manufacturing sector: scale, growth, and structural tailwinds
India has become one of the world's most dynamic manufacturing economies. Six major sectors now generate $1.2 trillion in revenue (FY25) — up from $640B during the COVID 2020 dip — and employ 27M workers (IBEF). GDP growth of 6–8% is the fastest of any major economy, powered by landmark policy: $26B in PLI schemes across 14 sectors, a $10B Semiconductor Mission, and a Foreign Trade Policy 2023 target of $1T in merchandise exports by FY30. Per IBEF, India's manufacturing sector has the potential to reach US$1 trillion by FY26. India is now the 2nd largest smartphone producer globally, with Apple, Samsung, Foxconn, and Tata running world-scale plants. The sector is in the middle of a foundational metamorphosis — from assembly-reliant to design-led manufacturing. IBEF Manufacturing NITI Aayog WEF Future of Jobs 2025

03 — Sector Selection
How we selected Automobile and FMCG from six sectors
Automobile and FMCG are India's largest manufacturing sectors by revenue. Both face systemic operational challenges — quality bottlenecks, demand forecasting errors, fragmented supply chains, and manual compliance processes — that AI can now solve at scale.

Sector Selection: 7-Dimension Scoring Matrix

Each sector was evaluated across seven categories. Each category is scored 1-5 per sector. The composite score determined which sectors to select for deep-dive AI opportunity analysis. Automobile and FMCG scored 1st and 2nd — driven by complementary strengths across different categories.

1. TAM for AI Automation

Total addressable market derived from knowledge worker payroll. Higher knowledge work density = larger AI opportunity.

2. Workforce Friction Index

Attrition rate as a proxy for workforce pain. High attrition = constant hiring = strong pull for AI. FMCG field force at 40-60% vs Metals at 8.6%.

3. Current Outsourcing Propensity

How much work is already delegated to third parties. High outsourcing = lower resistance to delegating work to AI. FMCG leads with extensive BPO usage.

4. AI & Digital Readiness

NASSCOM AI Adoption Index score. Measures data infrastructure, digital maturity, and executive AI awareness. ESDM and Auto lead; Metals lags.

5. Legal & Regulatory Friction

Union strength, labour law complexity, and regulatory barriers to workforce restructuring. Metals "Very High" friction (powerful unions); FMCG "Low-Moderate".

6. Financial Health & Margins

Net margin and revenue growth as indicators of technology investment capacity. Higher margin = more budget for AI. FMCG at 17%; Auto at 6.5%.

7. Overall AI Readiness Assessment

Composite judgement combining all six factors above, weighted by urgency. "Immediate" = deploy now; "Resistant" = multi-year transformation needed.

What is Enterprise Fraction and how is it derived?

India’s manufacturing sectors have thousands of companies, but B2B AI SaaS is sold to organized enterprises — companies with structured IT budgets, procurement teams, and digital infrastructure. SMEs and informal manufacturers are not the addressable market for enterprise software.

We derived the enterprise fraction using a bottom-up analysis of the top 20 listed companies in each sector via Screener.in. For each company, we extracted the employee cost ratio from their P&L statements, calculated knowledge worker payroll per enterprise, and computed how much of total sector KW payroll these enterprises represent.

Role-Based Automation Rate Explained

Not all knowledge worker roles are equally automatable. From our 258-role career stream library: Executive roles (10-12% of knowledge workers) — CXO/VP positions — will not be automated. Professional roles (31-42%) — data analysis, documentation, compliance — have the highest automation exposure (80-85%). Management roles (16-32%) — reporting, coordination — are partially automatable (60-65%). The weighted average across career streams gives effective automation rates of 56% (Auto) and 58% (FMCG) of knowledge worker work. For the final enterprise TAM, we apply a conservative 50% addressable factor and a further 50% AI price discount, reflecting the portion of automatable work where AI solutions are both functionally viable and priced below human labor.

07 — Methodology & Sources
Methodology, sources, and calculation rationale
A bottom-up approach: catalog every role, map it into a sequential workflow, identify AI-solvable problems per node, then calculate addressable market from actual payroll data.
01

Role Library

Cataloged 258 distinct job titles across 3 sectors from BLS, Mercer, Aon frameworks, and India-specific LinkedIn postings. Each role tagged with department, career stream, and collar type.

02

Workflow Mapping

Sequenced all roles into a 4-pillar value chain (Supply, Manufacturing, Demand, Support) using clinical workflow methodology borrowed from US healthcare.

03

Problem Identification

For each workflow node: identified current pain points, inefficiencies, and cost centers through industry research, analyst reports, and domain expertise.

04

AI Solution Mapping

Mapped each problem to specific AI capabilities (LLMs, computer vision, predictive analytics, agentic workflows) with real product examples from global markets.

05

TAM Calculation

TAM = 50% of addressable knowledge work payroll + process improvement value. Base data from IBEF, PLFS 2025, Alphasense, and Mordor Intelligence.

Methodology: top-down vs bottom-up reconciliation

The primary TAM calculation is bottom-up: we sum the individual TAMs of the 59 AI opportunity nodes (29 Auto + 30 FMCG). This yields $521M (Auto) + $511M (FMCG) = $1,032M (~$1.0B). The top-down derivation blocks above are a sanity check that independently approximates the same answer using sector-wide payroll, a 50% addressable factor, a 50% AI price discount, and an enterprise fraction tuned so both methods converge. The 50% AI price discount reflects that AI solutions must be priced below the cost of human labor to drive enterprise adoption. The 50% pricing discount is conservative relative to McKinsey's estimate of 40-50% of all knowledge work being automatable and WEF's projection of AI automating 40-50% of Indian knowledge work roles by 2030. The 50% captures both direct labor cost savings and process improvement value (quality, speed, accuracy gains) that AI delivers.
